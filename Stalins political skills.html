Was Stalin’s political skills the main factor being responsible for him becoming leader of the USSR in 1929?

Introduction

Joseph Stalin became General Secretary of the Communist Party[1] of the Soviet Union in 1922, however by 1929 Stalin was leader of the USSR he kept this position until his death in. Historians have disagreed on which factors mainly influenced this rise to power and how far his political skill was responsible for this. I will be primarily discussing three works published over a 40-year time period, with differing viewpoints on how he managed to do this The First viewpoint is known as intentionalism and is shown by Roy. A Medvedev in his work titled ‘Let History Judge the Origins and Consequences of Stalinism’ Medvedev argues that Stalin found his position by utilising his political finesse, Medvedev and other intentionalist writers believe that Stalin used his personality and skill to rise to power. The second viewpoint I will focus on is known as Structuralism and is the view of Russian Historian Oleg Khlevniuk as seen in his 2015 book ‘Stalin New Biography of a Dictator’ Structuralists such as Khlevniuk believe that Stalin managed to get to be the leader of the USSR primarily because of his position as General Secretary which became all important during the struggle for power. The third interpretation of why Stalin became leader of the USSR in 1929 is put forward in the book ‘Stalin the first in-depth biography based on explosive new documents from Russia’s secret archives’ by Edvard Radzinsky he shows how Stalin became leader of the USSR Primarily due to the failures and weaknesses of his political opponents. I will be discussing and analysing the authors I have mentioned interpretations of the question and then discuss my own beliefs on how Stalin managed to become the leader of the USSR by 1929 I personally take the view that Stalin managed to become leader of the USSR primarily  by utilising his position: General Secretary to form a powerbase from which he could more tightly control party affairs as well as increase his influence at a time when the General Secretary became an all-important position in the party.

First Interpretation

The first School of thought I’m going to discuss on how Stalin gained Primacy is known as intentionalism this is the idea that Stalin won against his opposition due to his personal qualities: manipulative and political skills, determination, and ruthlessness by these qualities Stalin managed achieve power by his own means. This idea is shown clearly by the soviet writer Roy Medvedev in his 1972 book ‘Let History Judge’ Medvedev has written many works which criticise Stalin usually from his Marxist perspective. When ‘Let History Judge’ was published Medvedev and many other intellectuals were trying to push a new reformist version of socialism meaning the book would be biased against Stalinism at a time when Soviet propagandists were trying to rehabilitate the former General Secretary, Because of the dissident viewpoint expressed by Medvedev being published abroad Medvedev was ejected from the Communist Party not joining back until 1989.

From the first page Medvedev makes clear his viewpoint where it states ‘Stalin displayed such great energy, organizational ability, and single minded will.’[2] From this alone it is clear Medvedev follows an intentionalist school of thought as Stalin is shown to be a skilled individual who would use these traits to gain power and influence.

Medvedev also discusses Stalin’s time in the party shortly after the civil war he discusses Stalin’s political skill in relation to Stalin’s relationship to Lenin saying how he would often object to Lenin’s policies but never join an official opposition group ‘At the decisive moment he always withdrew his objections and invariably appeared among the majority. It is difficult to believe that this was due only to the persuasiveness of Lenin’s arguments.’[3] From this it can be inferred Medvedev is saying Stalin would side with Lenin primarily to keep hold to the power he had this can be seen as political skill which goes in line with Medvedev’s intentionalist views.

During the chapter titled ‘Stalin’s struggle with the opposition’ Medvedev discusses Zinoviev’s plan to use Stalin to his advantage ‘Zinoviev aware of the mutual dislike between Stalin and Trotsky, assumed Stalin would be able to check Trotsky and open the way for his own rise to power’[4] because of this going against Lenin’s proposal to remove Stalin from the position of General Secretary Medvedev shows how Stalin used this situation to his advantage due to his personality ‘Stalin turned out to be much craftier than Zinoviev’ and goes onto explain Stalin’s use of his position; from this alone we can see Medvedev’s intentionalist viewpoint that Stalin was politically gifted and used his other traits to rise past his opposition.

At the time where the General Secretary’s position became increasingly important Medvedev shows Stalin as one of the few aware of these changes from which he cleverly formed a powerbase ‘Stalin took clever advantage of these changes. Relying on the many “fighters” and “practical” people in the party, steadily encouraging their resentment against the preponderant “theorists,” Stalin also formed a bloc with the Bukharin–Rykov group in the Politburo. Thus when Trotsky’s opposition was defeated in 1923-24, it was not Zinoviev but, rather surprisingly Stalin who acquired decisive influence in the CC.’[5] from this passage Medvedev shows explicitly Stalin’s skill to use his positioning effectively by which he could create a powerbase which allowed him to have a huge amount of influence in the Central Committee the intentionalist viewpoint explains Stalin was able to do this due to the trait of being ‘clever’ which Medvedev describes him to be once again showing how Medvedev believes Stalin used his political skill to become more powerful.

  .

Medvedev shows also surrounding happenings related to the Fourteenth party Congress Medvedev highlights Stalin’s character by showing how he used the struggle with the ‘New Opposition’ strengthening his power: ‘Stalin used the intra-party struggle to strengthen his personal position and power.’[6] Medvedev also comments on Stalin’s actions after the victory of the Politburo majority: ’Stalin hastened to consolidate his victory over the “new opposition” by organizational measures,’ keeping Zinoviev and Kamenev in the Politburo but removing them from other positions to lessen their influence and weaken their personal power as Bolshevik Oligarchs once again Medvedev is commenting on Stalin’s decision making among other personal traits and Political skill.

Regarding the ‘Struggle with the opposition’ Medvedevs references how Stalin wished to destroy his opponents one at a time and use his cunning to deceive the party of his true goals which was to split the party and expel members who disagreed with him. ‘Stalin sought to smash his political rivals completely and to establish one-man rule over the party’[7] and ‘he himself was a hypocrite who deceived the party and concealed his true goals. By first supporting Zinoviev and Kamenev against Trotsky, then Bukharin, Rykov and Tomskii against Zinoviev and Kamenev, Stalin did not unite the Party. He split it; he intensified existing disagreements, pushing toward the suppression and expulsion of those who disagreed with him.’

Another Work which Shows an intentionalist viewpoint is Robert Service’ Stalin a Biography Written in 2004 this book is very clearly intentionalist and like Medvedev also shows a belief that at least partly Stalin became leader of the USSR due to his political skill; This is shown rather clearly when he is discussing Stalin in relation to the Thirteenth Party Conference ‘He had the potential of a true leader. He was decisive, competent, confident and ambitious.’[8] Service then goes on to say how these qualities were ‘understood by other Central Committee members’[9] who allowed Stalin to lead the Charge against Trotsky at the Thirteenth Party Conference. Later in the book Service mentions how Stalin’s determination was a clear reason for how he was able to beat his opponents. ‘Stalin’s rivals quite underestimated his determination to prove them wrong in their little opinion of him’.[10] From this it's clear Service believes Stalin’s character traits were the main reason for him becoming leader of the USSR.

It is clear that Historians who follow the intentionalist view believe that Stalin used political skill along with his other traits to become leader of the USSR the fact that this view is very widespread and used among writers from both Soviet Russia and contemporary Britain shows that it is credible and not used to further a political viewpoint but only how Stalin’s character allowed him to gain power the fact Stalin is portrayed negatively in the early 1970s  being the reason why Medvedev was ejected from the Communist Party. However the Intentionalist school of thought does not focus much on the circumstances of the party as a whole making the view seem as though it leaves some things out. Damaging the persuasiveness of the viewpoint as a whole.

Second Interpretation

The second school of thought which I will be discussing will be Structuralism the view that Stalin’s rise to power was due to his position of General Secretary became all important giving him the ability crush his opposition and become leader in the events after the death of Lenin in 1924.

Explored in Oleg V. Khlevniuk’s 2015 work ‘Stalin New Biography of a Dictator where Khlevniuk challenges the traditional intentionalist view that Stalin became leader due to his personality, Khlevniuk is a Historian who has studied Stalinism for more than two decades he has been described by the dissident political scientist Gleb Pavlosky as ‘a leading Russian historian of Stalinism’ much of his work is based on declassified documents making him a very credible Historian.

Khlevniuk discusses how Stalin got the role of General Secretary; mentioning how it was given to Stalin in early 1922 in a ploy by Lenin to lessen the power of Trotsky and Trotskyists as ‘Trotsky was the only top Bolshevik how could rightfully claim to be a leader in his own right, not just a follower of Lenin.’[11] At the ‘Tenth Party Congress in early 1921, after intense maneuvering and considerable use of his authority, Lenin made sure his followers received a majority of the votes.’  Following this reshuffle of the party Khlevniuk mentions how Stalin was able to ‘play an increasingly important role in party affairs.’[12] Which was ‘formalised in 1922 when Stalin was appointed to the newly created post of general secretary of the Central Committee of the Russian Communist Party.’ It’s clear from this that Khlevniuk sees the General Secretary as a very important post since its creation which was explicitly created to limit the power of Trotskyists and as Stalin is said by Khlevniuk to be ‘one of Lenin’s key allied during this struggle’[13] Khlevniuk sees Lenin’s battle for primacy over the Bolsheviks as the primary reason for Stalin’s sharp gain of influence over party affairs, Which was further raised by the power he gained as General secretary giving him control over the leading structures of the communist party (the Central Committee apparat).

Khlevniuk goes on to mention how With Lenin’s Illness becoming worse a ‘troika’ of Stalin, Zinoviev and Kamenev was quickly gaining influence at this point Lenin believed that he would recover from his illness and was worried about the influence of the ‘troika’ especially with the tactic in play of isolating Trotsky, because of this ‘Lenin attempted to shift the balance of power, and Stalin was the easiest target.’[14] In summary after a few disputes including what is known as the ‘Georgian affair’ which caused a major break in the relationship of Lenin and Stalin after this the Politburo had the idea the idea of removing Stalin from his position as General Secretary however this would not happen with Lenin’s illness taking a turn for the worse in late 1923 after this the ‘Politburo … abandoned the idea of removing Stalin as General Secretary’ Khlevniuk mentions how this decision was not done by ‘Stalin’s friends’ but as a product of the struggle for power which continued following the death of Lenin in 1924.

Khlevniuk disregards the view that ‘the Bolshevik oligarchs who inherited power after Lenin’s demise underestimated Stalin and believed to be harmless and mediocre. Saying ‘this is not true. The members of the Politburo fully appreciated Lenin’s concerns about Stalin and the power he held as general secretary’[15] he mentions how they tried to limit his power but due to happenstance and Stalin’s maneuvering from his position he undermined the plans of his enemies from this it is clear to see Khlevniuk’s view of structuralism showing how it wasn’t the mistakes of Stalin’s enemies that kept his position it was the position itself which gave Stalin the power.

In regards to Stalin’s opposition Khlevniuk mentions how following the Fourteenth Party Congress in December 1925 the majority of the politburo were against the oppositionists: Trotsky, Zinoviev and Kamenev who had unified in a ‘marriage of convenience’ however he shows his structuralist view in saying ‘all power flowed downstream from the Politburo’[16] this particularly shows Khlevniuk’s structuralist views as Stalin controlled party membership from his position as General Secretary. After the oppositionists gathered out of sight of the authorities in June 1926 Stalin branded the group as an ‘illegal organisation’ and Zinoviev was removed from the Politburo, by October 1926 Trotsky and Kamenev were also removed from the Politburo.

Khlevniuk makes it clear by 1927 there was negligible competition for primacy in the communist party as ‘only one power hungry member remained in the Politburo: Stalin’ shows how Khlevniuk believes Stalin unstoppable by this point. He then summarises: ‘In the pivotal post of general secretary, Stalin used the battle with the left opposition to strengthen his position. The Schism within the party permitted him to play the role of the preserver of Lenin’s legacy and strengthened his control over the party apparat and state security. These advantages did not assure him victory, but they shifted the odds in his favour.’[17] From this summary it is very clear to see that Khlevniuk sees the fact the role of general secretary became all important as the main reason for his rise and success against his opponents in the struggle for power as it gave him a huge advantage.

Another work in which the author shows a Structuralist view is Sheila Fitzpatrick’s 2015 book titled ‘On Stalin’s Team the Years of Living Dangerously in Soviet Politics in which a Structuralist view is very clear as she describes how Stalin’s position helped him gain the position of leader of the USSR this is shown first when she talks about the power Stalin had as General Secretary, ‘Stalin had concentrated enormous power in his hands as the Party’s General Secretary.’[18]  she also mentions how oppositionists to the party majority which was influenced by Stalin’s control of the party at a low level were harassed out of making points allowing Stalin to become more powerful ‘At Party congresses oppositionists were now mercilessly heckled.’[19] The fact this is what is primarily mentioned shows Structuralists Primarily belief the party structure to be the reason how Stalin became leader of the USSR

In Conclusion the Structuralist view is clearly showing the position Stalin had as General secretary as the main reason for his rise to leader though it does seem like Stalin’s political ability is referenced to an extent I also find the view rather persuasive as historians that follow it such as Khlevniuk reference many documents and events as well as this it is a belief that is not outdated post glasnost, like the school of thoughts many soviet writers have.

Third Interpretation

The view of Edvard Radzinsky is that the main reason for Stalin’s success in the struggle for power against his opposition was the weaknesses and mistakes of his opponents especially Trotsky, Zinoviev and Kamenev who in the mid to late 1920s made many mistakes that would allow Stalin to gain power and influence eventually leading to their expulsion from the Politburo and Stalin’s single man unchallenged leadership of the USSR he show’s this in his 1996 book titled ‘Stalin The First in-depth biography based on explosive new documents from Russia’s secret Archives.’

Radzinsky first criticises Trotsky following Lenin’s death for believing he could gain primacy quickly ‘His calculation no was that he could take the conference by storm. But this was the twentieth century: the age of individuals was over.’[20] From this it can be seen that Radzinsky sees this miscalculation from Trotsky as the start of his downfall as he was isolated interestingly compared to other writers Radzinsky does not blame reshuffling of the party as a reason for this isolation but Trotsky’s personality.

Radzinsky goes on further to show that the actions of Trotsky, Zinoviev and Kamenev based on Lenin’s testament was another key moment in the securing of Stalin’s power as General Secretary which allowed him to increase his personal powerbase; as the leaders voted to keep Stalin in his position after he offered his resignation which he offered to follow the will of Lenin; the leaders would do this in a bid to make sure no one of them could gain the position as they simply saw Stalin as irrelevant this would play directly into Stalin’s hand ‘It happened exactly as he expected: they all – Trotsky, Kamenev and Zinoviev, because they hated each other – voted unanimously that he should remain’[21]. Radzinsky clearly references this to show how the failure of the opposition as a reason for Stalin’s continually increasing power starting in 1924.

Radzinsky then goes on to talk about how Trotsky was greatly weakened by the troika of Stalin, Kamenev, Zinoviev and puts the blame squarely on Trotsky himself showing how Trotsky’s nature was what caused him this problem when referring to Trotsky’s beliefs about the opposition he was facing he says ‘he Trotsky was the October leader, that Zinoviev and Kamenev were cowards, that Stalin was simply irrelevant’[22] ‘This was suicide.’[23]  This last comment is particularly helpful to infer how incompetent Trotsky was politically at this time.

Radzinsky continually uses the weaknesses of Stalin’s opponents to show how he got his power only also giving credit to the powerbase Stalin had built up with which he made his personal political enemies, oppositionist enemies to the Politburo ‘Eminent Bolsheviks who had once been Lenin’s comrades-in-arms and were know “oppositionists.” ’[24] this shows how due to Stalin’s power base which he had created due to his position of General Secretary. Also on the topic of the oppositionists he highlights their failure (Trotsky, Zinoviev and Kamenev) to see the reality of the situation they were in citing that they believed such prominent figures as themselves would win over the Party by a united presence alone: ‘ they suffered from the peculiar Russian disease of naïve romanticism. They believed that the three of them only had to appear together and the Party would immediately remember the heroic past and fall in behind its former leaders. They refused to see that the membership had long been eating out of the new potentate’s hand, that the overwhelming majority of majority of the bureaucrats who now managed the party had been installed by Stalin,’[25] this shows Stalin’s opposition by this point was doomed to fail due to Stalin’s changes he made as general secretary and more so due to their own failure to recognise the effects these changes would have had.

Radzinsky also talks about the effects of the underground press which was set up for oppositionists the underground activity was declared illegal by Stalin allowing for a purge of Trotskyists from the party and the expulsion of Trotsky and Zinoviev in November ‘Trotsky was obliged to set up a underground press to print his program.’ [26]‘This is just what Stalin had been waiting for. The underground press became the excuse for the immediate expulsion of Trotsky’s supporters from the party.’ This shows how Radzinsky again shows Trotsky’s obligation to have an underground press would severely damage the oppositionists leading to its leaders being removed from power allowing for Stalin to consolidate his rule and end up leader of the USSR by 1929.

My thoughts on the value of the work of Radzinsky is that it is fairly valuable being a contemporary non mainstream view and he has cited many sources though unlike other works it doesn’t at all mention what other historians mention such as the personal qualities of Stalin or the fact that he had some form of political skill making it not as persuasive as for example Khlevniuk’s work.

Evaluation and Comparison of Viewpoints and Works and Conclusion as well as My Personal Opinion.

The main three works I have read for this have all been informative and have offered very different points of view into how Stalin was able to become the leader of the USSR by 1929.

The first work of the main works I have mentioned was Medvedev’s ‘Let History Judge’ this book portrayed Stalin’s character very negatively however what it did do was clearly show the authors view on how Stalin managed to become leader which was through his personal skills and traits from this Medvedev clearly believed that Stalin used his political skill to a strong extent to become leader of the USSR this intentionalist school of thought contrasts with the other two works sharply as they both had very different ideas of the primary reason which resulted in Stalin’s primacy; Medvedev’s books was also published before Glasnost so although it does not follow the mainstream soviet view of the early late 1960s and early 1970s Medvedev does not have all the information he could have had making his book less reliable than that of example Khlevniuk though the intentionalist view is not limited to soviet writers as seen in Service’ Biography of Stalin from 2004 I also found the scope of ‘Let History Judge’ to be limited in regard to the struggle for power when compared to the other works I have read only really focusing on Stalin and the Bolshevik Oligarchs rather than the party as a whole which again when compared to Khlevniuk and even Radzinksky’s work makes ‘Let History Judge’ seem much more unreliable and less persuasive.

Khlevniuk being a structuralist primarily beliefs that the structure of the Party was the reason for Stalin becoming leader he explores this belief in his book ‘Stalin New Biography of a Dictator’; he and other Structuralists such as Fitzpatrick believe that Stalin holding the position of General Secretary gave him the advantage he needed to win the power struggle after the death of Lenin Structuralists attribute some of Stalin’s success to his political skill they do not believe that Stalin relied on luck as much as he relied on the position to create a powerbase which he could use to his advantage, what makes the Structuralist view so persuasive is that it is very common post Glasnost meaning many of the more contemporary authors that use this argument are knowledgeable to documents that for example soviet writers like Medvedev weren’t as well as this the view explores lots of the politics sounding the party and not just what effected Stalin it can also be argued however that the Structuralist view found in Khlevniuk’s work unjustifiably gives too much credit to Stalin’s skill especially when compared with the revisionist view that it was a social and cultural change in the new party membership that allowed Stalin to become leader downplaying the importance of his political skill.

Radzinsky believed in the idea that Stalin’s opposition’s failure was the main reason for his success in the power struggle as shown in his book and after the fall of the main Bolshevik oligarchs of Trotsky, Kamenev and Zinoviev he had no real opposition and because of this he essentially relied on luck to get into power although Radzinsky does cite a lot of information I believe he focuses on the wrong things his view is much less persuasive than Khlevniuk’s or Medvedev’s view due to the fact he seems to completely ignore Stalin’s own skill that clearly helped him off the back of the failure of his opponents as well as the powerbase Stalin managed to acquire; more so than Khlevniuk’s work Radzinksky’s book is contrasted by the revisionist view as seen in Trotsky’s work titled ‘A Revolution Betrayed’ where Trotsky focuses on the change of the views of the membership being the main reason for the success of Stalin in the power Struggle as he was the only old Bolshevik that would help them in their ‘narrow minded view’[27] as he explains ‘every revolution … has been followed by a counter revolution’ meaning that a change in the soviet view was the main reason for Stalin’s eventual position as leader rather than luck even this view which doesn’t take into account internal politics seems to me personally more persuasive than the view of Radzinsky.

My personal view is that Stalin becoming leader was primarily due to the importance and all powerful nature that the position of General Secretary gave him with this he was able to form a powerbase which allowed him to control the views of the Party  allowing him to isolate his opposition and eventually become leader I mostly follow a structuralist view as shown in Khlevniuk’s work with liberal/intentionalist elements also, I believe that Stalin used his political skill to manipulate events such as the Fourteenth Party Conference and destroy his opposition. I disagree with Radzinksky’s view which primarily focused on the mistakes of Stalin’s opponents as it contrasts heavily with my personal view as well as this I think Medvedev’s view focused on Stalin personally too much as that book was designed primarily to focus on Stalin’s personal traits which isn’t entirely congruent with the power struggle when compared to the structuralist view, however I equally disagree with some elements of the structuralist view as it seems to downplay any effect Stalin’s opposition would have had and it is very hard to argue that figures such as Trotsky, Kamenev and Zinoviev didn’t provide enough opposition to make it so Stalin could just ignore them with his position of General Secretary so it makes more sense that it only ‘shifted the odds in his favour’ in the words of Khlevniuk.
